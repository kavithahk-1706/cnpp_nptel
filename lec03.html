Circuit Switching & Packet Switching

▶ watch lecture
Lecture Overview

This lecture covers the two fundamental architectural choices for building switched networks: circuit switching and packet switching. Understanding this distinction is essential — it represents the core design decision that separates the traditional telephone network from the modern internet. The lecture also examines the two variants of packet switching (datagram and virtual circuit) and the trade-offs associated with each approach.

Switched Networks — The Problem Being Solved ↗ ~01:15
A switched network is a communication network in which data is routed between end devices (stations) through a series of intermediate switching nodes. The fundamental challenge is getting data from a source to a destination when there is no direct physical connection between every pair of users — the intermediate nodes are responsible for making the forwarding decisions.

All long-distance communication relies on this model. The two fundamentally different philosophies for implementing it are circuit switching and packet switching.

Circuit Switching ↗ ~04:00
In circuit switching, a dedicated, end-to-end communication path is established before any data transfer begins. All intermediate nodes along the path reserve the necessary resources (switching capacity, channel bandwidth) for the exclusive use of this connection. The path remains reserved for the entire duration of the communication, regardless of whether data is actively being sent.

The Three Phases of a Circuit-Switched Connection ↗ ~05:30

Phase 1 — Path Establishment: A signaling process propagates through the network, reserving resources at each intermediate node to construct the end-to-end circuit. No data transfer can begin until this phase completes successfully.

Phase 2 — Data Transfer: Once the circuit is established, data flows along the dedicated path at a fixed, guaranteed rate. The connection behaves like a private wire — its capacity is unaffected by other traffic in the network.

Phase 3 — Disconnection: When the communication ends, a teardown signal is sent, releasing the reserved resources at each node along the path so they become available for other connections.

Blocking vs. Non-Blocking Architecture ↗ ~14:40

Consider a switch with 10 ports, all of which are currently handling active connections. If an 11th connection attempt arrives, a blocking switch will reject it — no additional resources are available. A non-blocking switch, by contrast, is designed with sufficient internal capacity to always accommodate any new connection request, regardless of current load. Real-world network switches strive for non-blocking designs, though this comes at significantly higher hardware cost.

Advantages and Disadvantages of Circuit Switching

guaranteed bandwidth no congestion mid-call in-order delivery guaranteed suitable for voice and real-time streams
setup delay before any data flows bandwidth wasted during idle periods fixed data rate — both ends must match single node failure destroys the entire circuit highly inefficient for bursty data traffic
The central inefficiency of circuit switching: reserved bandwidth is held for the entire duration of a connection, even when no data is being transmitted. A phone call with a silent pause still holds the channel — that capacity cannot be used by anyone else. This is why circuit switching is poorly suited to data networks, where traffic is bursty and highly irregular rather than continuous.
Packet Switching ↗ ~20:06
In packet switching, the source breaks data into small, discrete units called packets. Each packet contains a portion of the data along with a header carrying control information (addressing, routing metadata, sequence numbers). Packets are injected into the network independently and may travel through different intermediate nodes before being reassembled at the destination.

The key forwarding mechanism at every intermediate node is store-and-forward: the node receives the entire packet, buffers it briefly in memory, makes a routing decision, and then transmits it toward the next hop. There is no pre-reservation of resources and no dedicated path.

Advantages and Disadvantages of Packet Switching

bandwidth consumed only when data is being sent multiple packet streams share the same link dynamically traffic can be rerouted around failed or congested nodes packets can be assigned different priorities accommodates communicating devices with different data rates
no guaranteed bandwidth packets may arrive out of order packets can be lost or excessively delayed variable delay (jitter) depending on congestion receiver must handle reordering and loss recovery

Two Variants of Packet Switching ↗ ~27:17

1. Datagram

Each packet is treated as a fully independent unit. Every packet carries the complete destination address in its header, and every intermediate node independently decides how to forward it based on current network conditions. Because packets make their own routing decisions, different packets belonging to the same communication may follow entirely different paths through the network — and consequently may arrive at the destination out of order, be duplicated, or be lost entirely. The responsibility for handling these anomalies rests entirely with the end systems (specifically, the transport layer protocols running on them).

No setup phase is required. Each packet is self-contained and self-routing. This is the model used by the internet at the IP layer — IP is a datagram protocol.

2. Virtual Circuit ↗ ~27:34

A logical route is pre-established before packets are sent, similar to circuit switching. However, the path is not truly dedicated — it may be shared with other virtual circuits simultaneously. Each packet carries a compact VCI (Virtual Circuit Identifier) rather than the full destination address. At each intermediate node, a lookup table maps the incoming (port, VCI) pair to an outgoing (port, VCI) pair, and the packet is forwarded accordingly.

How VCI forwarding works at a node: The switch maintains a translation table. When a packet arrives on a given port with a given VCI, the switch looks up the corresponding output port and new VCI, rewrites the VCI in the packet header, and forwards it. VCIs are locally scoped — the same VCI number on different links can refer to completely different virtual circuits.
example VCI table at a switch
in: port 1 VCI 14 → out: port 3 VCI 66
in: port 1 VCI 77 → out: port 2 VCI 14
in: port 2 VCI 22 → out: port 3 VCI 22

Because all packets belonging to a virtual circuit follow the same pre-established path, they are guaranteed to arrive in order. Forwarding is also faster than datagram routing since the routing decision is made only once at setup — each subsequent packet is processed via a simple table lookup. However, if any intermediate node along the path fails, all virtual circuits passing through it are destroyed and must be re-established.
