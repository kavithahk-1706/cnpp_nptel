Layer 1: Physical Layer – II

Data vs signals, analog vs digital, signal properties, transmission impairments, and theoretical data rate limits (Nyquist & Shannon). Heavy on formulas — make sure you can apply them, not just state them.

Data and Signals

Everything processed by your system is data — ultimately a stream of 1s and 0s. But the physical medium underneath can only carry electromagnetic signals, so data must be transformed before transmission.

Analog
Data Continuous values (e.g. voice, temperature)
Signal Infinite values within a range; smooth waveform
Use Periodic analog signals are common in comms
Digital
Data Discrete states — 0 or 1 (or more levels)
Signal Limited number of values; stepped waveform
Use Non-periodic digital signals are common in comms
In data communication, we predominantly use periodic analog signals and non-periodic digital signals.
Properties of Periodic Analog Signals

A periodic (sine) wave is fully described by three parameters:

Amplitude
Peak value of the signal — the maximum it reaches on the positive (or minimum on the negative) side. Represents signal strength/power.
Frequency (f)
Number of complete cycles per second, measured in Hz. Inversely related to period T: f = 1/T and T = 1/f.
Phase
Offset of the wave relative to time zero — how much the waveform is "shifted" from its reference start position. Measured in degrees.
Visual hint: The slide at 09:54 shows three sine waves with identical amplitude and frequency but phases of 0°, 90°, and 180° — worth visualising. Adding out-of-phase waves produces a composite result that differs from simply stacking in-phase waves.
Composite Signals & Signal Domains

When multiple sine waves are added together, the result is a composite signal. Any composite signal can be represented in two ways:

Time Domain
Signal amplitude plotted against time. Shows what the waveform actually looks like moment to moment.
Frequency Domain
Signal amplitude plotted against frequency. Shows which frequency components are present and their respective amplitudes. A single sine wave = one spike.

Fourier analysis is the mathematical bridge: any composite signal can be decomposed into its individual sine wave components. A three-wave composite of frequencies 0, 8, 16 Hz appears as three separate spikes in the frequency domain.

Bandwidth of a Composite Signal

Bandwidth is the range of frequencies a signal occupies:

BW = fhighest − flowest
Example: Human voice spans ~0–4 kHz → BW = 4 kHz. POTS telephone lines are also designed for this range.

Why does this matter? A channel (physical medium) has its own frequency-passing characteristics — it may only allow a certain band of frequencies through. Your signal's bandwidth must fit within the channel's bandwidth, otherwise data is lost.

Periodic vs Non-Periodic Digital Signals in Frequency Domain

Both have theoretically infinite bandwidth. However, periodic digital signals have discrete frequencies (spikes at harmonics: f, 3f, 5f, 7f…), while non-periodic digital signals have a continuous frequency spectrum. In practice, only the dominant harmonics are transmitted — the high-frequency tail contributes little to reconstruction.

Digital Signals — Levels & Bit Rate

Signal Levels

A digital signal doesn't have to be just 2 levels (0V / +5V). It can have multiple voltage levels, each encoding more than one bit per signal element.

2 levels → 1 bit per level  (log₂ 2 = 1)
4 levels → 2 bits per level  (log₂ 4 = 2)
8 levels → 3 bits per level  (log₂ 8 = 3)
L levels → log₂ L bits per level

More levels = more data packed per signal element, but also more susceptible to noise (finer distinctions are harder to tell apart at the receiver). Not always feasible in noisy channels.

Bit Rate

Bit rate = number of bits transmitted per second (bps). It is a property of the data stream, not the signal directly.

Example calculations from lecture

100 pages/min text → 100 × 24 × 80 × 8 = 1.536 Mbps
Digitized voice (4 kHz, 8 bits/sample) → 2 × 4000 × 8 = 64 kbps
HDTV (1920×1080, 30fps, 24 bits/pixel) → 1920×1080×30×24 ≈ 1.5 Gbps (compressed to 20–40 Mbps in practice)
Nyquist sampling theorem (for digitised analog): to faithfully represent an analog signal of bandwidth B Hz, you must sample at 2B samples per second. That's why voice at 4 kHz → 8000 samples/sec.

Bit Length

Bit length is the physical distance a single bit occupies in the medium while in transit:

Bit Length = Propagation Speed × Bit Duration
Transmission Impairments

Real media are not perfect. The signal received is not identical to the signal sent. Three categories:

1. Attenuation

Signal loses energy as it travels through the medium (overcomes resistance). Amplitude decreases with distance. Fixed using amplifiers (analog) or regenerators/repeaters (digital — preferred since they reconstruct a clean signal rather than amplifying noise too).

Attenuation is measured in decibels (dB):
Attenuation (dB) = 10 · log₁₀(P₂ / P₁)
If power is halved: 10 · log₁₀(0.5) = 10 × (−0.3) = −3 dB
→ A loss of 3 dB ≡ losing half the signal power.

2. Distortion

Signal changes shape. Occurs in composite signals because each frequency component travels through the medium at a slightly different speed, arriving at the receiver with different delays. This causes phase shifts — the components are no longer in sync at the receiver, and when recombined, they produce a corrupted version of the original composite signal.

3. Noise

Type Cause
Thermal Random motion of electrons in the wire — creates spurious signals never sent by the transmitter
Induced Interference from external EM sources — motors, appliances, power lines nearby
Crosstalk One wire acts as a sending antenna, adjacent wire acts as a receiving antenna — signals bleed across wires
Impulse Short, high-energy spikes from lightning or power surges — brief but highly destructive to data
Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)

SNR quantifies how much stronger the signal is compared to the noise floor. Used to determine the theoretical maximum achievable bit rate.

SNR = Average Signal Power / Average Noise Power

SNRdB = 10 · log₁₀(SNR)

High SNR → signal dominates noise → reliable transmission
Low SNR → noise dominates → data corruption
Signal = 10 mW, Noise = 1 μW → SNR = 10,000 → SNRdB = 40 dB
Data Rate Limits

Three factors govern achievable data rate: bandwidth available, number of signal levels, and channel noise quality (SNR). Two theoretical formulas bound what's possible:

Nyquist — Noiseless Channel
Formula BitRate = 2 × B × log₂ L
Where B = bandwidth (Hz), L = number of signal levels
Tells you How many signal levels to use once you fix the target bit rate
Limit No noise assumed — purely theoretical ceiling for a given L
Shannon — Noisy Channel
Formula C = B × log₂(1 + SNR)
Where B = bandwidth (Hz), SNR = signal-to-noise ratio
Tells you Absolute upper bound regardless of how many levels you use
Limit If SNR → 0 (pure noise), C → 0. No data can get through.

Practical workflow: use Shannon first, then Nyquist

Shannon gives the upper bound on capacity. Since achieving the absolute maximum is impractical (too sensitive to noise), you pick a lower, realistic target bit rate, then use Nyquist to find the required number of signal levels to achieve it.

Worked example (from lecture)

Given B = 1 MHz, SNR = 63
Shannon upper limit C = 10⁶ × log₂(1+63) = 10⁶ × log₂(64) = 10⁶ × 6 = 6 Mbps
Choose target rate 4 Mbps (safer margin below Shannon limit)
Nyquist for L 4 × 10⁶ = 2 × 10⁶ × log₂ L → log₂ L = 2 → L = 4 levels
When SNR is very high, the Shannon formula simplifies to an approximation:
C ≈ B × (SNRdB / 3)

Concept Summary

Data vs Signal: Data is what you want to send; a signal is the EM representation that actually travels through the medium. Both can be analog or digital.

Signal properties: Periodic analog signals are characterised by amplitude (strength), frequency (repetition rate), and phase (start offset). Adding signals with different phases yields a composite waveform.

Bandwidth: The frequency range a signal occupies. A channel has a finite bandwidth — signals whose bandwidth exceeds the channel's passband get distorted or clipped.

Signal levels & bit rate: More voltage levels per signal element = more bits per baud, but also more noise-sensitive. Bit rate calculations are straightforward multiplications once you know samples/sec and bits/sample.

Transmission impairments: Attenuation (energy loss — fixed by amplifiers/regenerators), Distortion (phase mismatch in composite signals — caused by different propagation speeds per frequency), and Noise (thermal, induced, crosstalk, impulse).

SNR: The ratio of signal power to noise power. High SNR = clean channel. Expressed in dB. Critical for computing capacity limits.

Nyquist vs Shannon: Nyquist gives the theoretical max bit rate in a noiseless channel for a given number of signal levels. Shannon gives the absolute capacity ceiling for a noisy channel given SNR. In practice: use Shannon to find the ceiling, pick a safer target below it, use Nyquist to find required signal levels.
