Lecture 34 Medium Priority

Internet QoS – IV: Traffic Scheduling

Queuing disciplines for multi-class scheduling at intermediate routers, and congestion avoidance via Random Early Detection (RED).

QoS Architecture — Quick Recap

The full QoS pipeline at a router involves four stages in sequence:

Stage 1
Admission Control

Decides whether to let a new flow enter the network based on available capacity and SLA commitments.

Stage 2
Classification & Marking

Tags each packet with a QoS class (e.g. red = VoIP, green = VoD, blue = FTP) based on the SLA of the originating flow.

Stage 3
Traffic Policing & Shaping

Enforces rate limits and smooths out bursts at intermediate routers using token bucket / leaky bucket mechanisms.

Stage 4
Traffic Scheduling ← today

Determines the order in which marked packets are forwarded out of the router. This is the mechanism that actually enforces differentiated QoS.

Multi-Class Scheduling

Packets are classified into traffic classes, each requiring different treatment. The scheduler must serve these classes from separate queues rather than a single shared buffer.

Class Priority Traffic Type Examples Key Requirement
Class 1 High Delay-sensitive VoIP Minimum queuing delay
Class 2 Medium Bandwidth-hungry VoD, IPTV Guaranteed bandwidth
Class 3 Low Best-effort HTTP, FTP No specific guarantee
SLA (Service Level Agreement): A contract between the user and the network provider specifying the QoS to be guaranteed for a particular flow or application. Think of it as the fine print behind every data plan — "I pay for VoIP priority, treat my packets accordingly."
Scheduling Strategy 1 — Priority Queuing

Incoming traffic is classified and placed into separate priority queues (High / Medium / Low). The scheduler always drains the highest non-empty queue before serving lower ones.

Non-Preemptive Priority Scheduling

The scheduler cycles through queues in round-robin order. Once it starts serving a queue, it drains it completely before checking a lower-priority queue. It does not interrupt ongoing service even if higher-priority packets arrive mid-cycle.

Order of service: Drain all of High → drain all of Medium → drain all of Low → repeat from High.

Preemptive Priority Scheduling

The scheduler can be interrupted mid-service. If it is draining the Medium queue and a packet arrives in the High queue, it immediately abandons Medium and jumps to High. It returns to Medium only once High is empty again.

Starvation risk: Under heavy high-priority load, the Low queue may never get served — packets there wait indefinitely. The advantage is near-zero delay and jitter for high-priority traffic, which makes it ideal for VoIP and network control packets.
Airport analogy: Think of the High queue as the VIP gate — passengers are waved through immediately, even pulling agents away from other counters if needed. At off-peak hours it doesn't matter since everyone gets served anyway. The problem (and the value of scheduling) only shows up under heavy load.

Network control packets — OSPF hellos, BGP keepalives, routing updates — are canonical examples of traffic that must go into the high-priority queue. Delaying them can destabilise the entire network.

Scheduling Strategy 2 — Custom Queuing

Different queues are assigned different sizes (proportional to the bandwidth share desired). The scheduler applies simple round-robin across all queues regardless of their fill level.

Queue 1
Size: 0.3 (30%)

30% of link capacity guaranteed to this class during peak load.

Queue 2
Size: 0.2 (20%)

20% of link capacity guaranteed.

Queue 3
Size: 0.5 (50%)

50% of link capacity guaranteed. Larger queue = more space = more packets served per round.

Why this works: During peak load all queues are perpetually full. Since the scheduler visits each queue once per round and each queue can hold a proportional number of packets, larger queues contribute more traffic per round. Queue size directly maps to bandwidth share — this is what "guaranteed bandwidth" means here.

Custom queuing works like a transmission window — each class is allocated a slice of the output capacity. Contrast with priority queuing where one class can starve others; here every class is guaranteed its share regardless of what others are doing.

Use case: Video streaming (VoD, IPTV) — applications that need a consistent guaranteed chunk of bandwidth rather than the lowest possible latency.

Scheduling Strategy 3 — Weighted Fair Queuing (WFQ)

WFQ accounts for variable packet sizes and aims to allocate equal (or weighted) bandwidth to each class regardless of how large individual packets are.

Fairness Mechanism

Instead of sending one packet per queue per round (which would bias towards classes with smaller packets), WFQ normalises by data volume. Consider three flows with packet sizes 1, 4, and 2 units:

Flow (colour) Packet Size Packets Sent Total Data Served
Blue 1 unit 4 packets 4 units ✓
Red 4 units 1 packet 4 units ✓
Green 2 units 2 packets 4 units ✓
All three flows receive equal total bandwidth across a scheduling round — this is fairness. Queues can also be assigned weights so that bandwidth is allocated proportionally (e.g. 2:1:1) rather than equally.
Combining Strategies — Multilevel Queue Scheduling

In practice, a single scheduling strategy is rarely sufficient. A two-level approach is commonly used:

Level 1 — Priority Queuing
Classifier separates traffic into priority classes (P1, P2, P3). High-priority classes are always served before lower ones. Ensures delay-sensitive traffic (VoIP) is not blocked by bulk traffic.
Level 2 — WFQ within each class
Within each priority class, packets of different sizes are served using WFQ. Ensures that within P1 (say), a flow with large packets doesn't starve flows with small packets. Enforces fairness at the intra-class level.
The two levels are orthogonal: Level 1 handles inter-class priority, Level 2 handles intra-class fairness. Together they give you both.

Both are necessary. Congestion avoidance shields high-priority (inelastic) traffic from entering congestion. But if low-priority (elastic/TCP) traffic does hit congestion, TCP's own congestion control must kick in to recover. Neither replaces the other.

The dominance problem: Elastic traffic will aggressively increase its rate until the network is congested. Inelastic traffic has no mechanism to back off. Result: elastic flows crowd out inelastic flows, causing packet drops in the traffic that can least tolerate them. Congestion avoidance exists specifically to prevent this.
Note: YouTube (pre-buffered video) is not real-time. It uses adaptive bitrate streaming and TCP — it is elastic. YouTube Live, VoIP, and video calls are real-time inelastic traffic.
Random Early Detection (RED)

RED is the standard congestion avoidance algorithm used at routers. It detects impending congestion early by watching the average queue length, and preemptively drops packets before the queue fills completely.

Core Idea

The only way to prevent congestion is to drop packets from flows that are sending too aggressively before the queue overflows. RED does this probabilistically and early — hence "Random" and "Early Detection."

Decision Logic (per incoming packet)

Average Queue Length (k) Zone Action
k < MinThresh Safe Enqueue the packet — no risk of congestion
MinThresh ≤ k ≤ MaxThresh Warning Calculate drop probability d(k). Drop randomly if d(k) is high; else enqueue
k > MaxThresh Danger Drop the packet — congestion imminent, must discard to prevent overflow

Drop Probability Formula

d(k) = Max_p × ( k − MinThresh ) / ( MaxThresh − MinThresh )
k → current average queue length
Max_p → maximum allowed drop probability (configured parameter)
MinThresh → lower queue length threshold — RED begins operating above this
MaxThresh → upper threshold — above this, drop probability = 1 (drop everything)

Drop Probability Graph

The graph of d(k) vs average queue size (k) looks like this:

k < MinThresh→d(k) = 0 (safe zone, flat line at 0)
MinThresh ≤ k ≤ MaxThresh→d(k) rises linearly from 0 to Max_p
k > MaxThresh→d(k) = 1 (drop all — virtual drop tail)
The linear ramp between thresholds is key: RED does not suddenly drop everything when congestion appears. It applies increasing pressure gradually, giving elastic flows (TCP) the chance to detect isolated drops and reduce their rate on their own — without waiting for a full queue overflow.

Why Random Drops?

TCP's congestion control triggers on 3 duplicate ACKs or a timeout. A single isolated random drop produces only 1 duplicate ACK — not enough to trigger fast retransmit. So early in the warning zone, RED's random drops are "soft signals" to individual flows. As the queue fills further and more packets are dropped, TCP eventually detects congestion and reduces its rate. This is intentional — it gives flows a chance to self-correct before drastic action is taken.

Summary of RED's two-word name: Early = acts before the queue overflows by monitoring average queue length as a proxy for congestion. Random = drops are probabilistic (not deterministic), distributing the burden across flows and avoiding synchronisation of TCP backoffs.
Concept Summary

Scheduling Strategies

Priority Queuing
Multiple queues ordered by priority. Higher queues are drained first. Non-preemptive = full drain then move; preemptive = jump back immediately on new high-priority arrival. Risk: low-priority starvation.
Custom Queuing
Queues of different sizes, round-robin scheduling. Larger queue = higher bandwidth share. Guarantees minimum bandwidth per class. No starvation. Suitable for video/bandwidth-hungry applications.
Weighted Fair Queuing
Normalises service by data volume rather than packet count. Ensures equal (or weighted) bandwidth across flows with variable packet sizes. Can be combined with priority queuing at a second scheduling level.

Congestion Avoidance (RED)

RED monitors average queue length at the router and probabilistically drops packets once the queue enters a warning zone. Drop probability increases linearly from 0 at MinThresh to Max_p at MaxThresh, then to 1 above MaxThresh. It is class-aware: higher-priority flows can be given a lower drop probability, pushing congestion onto lower-priority (elastic) flows that are better equipped to handle it via TCP's own congestion control.

The big picture: Scheduling controls which packets go out first (order). RED controls whether a packet enters the queue at all (admission under load). Together with TCP congestion control, the three form the complete QoS response mechanism at the network layer.
