Physical Layer — III: Encoding, Modulation & Multiplexing

Overview of how data (analog or digital) is converted into transmittable signals, and how multiple signals share a single channel. Heavy communication-engineering territory — the course covers this at a survey level, not deep math.

Encoding & Modulation — Basics

Encoding vs Modulation

Encoding is the process of converting data into a specific format for efficient transmission. Decoding is the reverse — extracting the original data from the transmitted format.

Modulation is a specific kind of encoding where the source signal (called the baseband signal or modulating signal, m(t)) is impressed onto a high-frequency carrier signal of frequency fc, producing a modulated signal s(t).

Why modulate at all? Every transmission medium has a usable frequency range it can carry faithfully. Raw data signals often sit in a low-frequency range that the medium attenuates heavily. A carrier frequency shifts the signal into the range the channel supports, maximising SNR at the receiver.

Modulation operates on one or more of the three parameters of the carrier signal: amplitude, frequency, or phase.

Four Encoding / Modulation Scenarios

Any combination of data type and signal type is possible. The technique chosen depends on the medium and application:

Data Type Signal Type Notes
Digital Digital Simpler, cheaper equipment. Voltage pulses directly represent bits.
Analog Digital Digitisation of voice/video. Enables use of modern digital transmission networks.

Analog Analog Baseband (e.g. telephone lines) or modulated (AM/FM radio). Enables FDM.

The receiver must know both timing (when each signal element begins and ends) and the signal levels in use. Three factors govern how successfully a signal is interpreted:

Encoding Schemes (Digital → Digital)

Raw voltage-level signals often perform poorly on real media. Encoding schemes remap bit patterns to signal elements to improve SNR, ease synchronisation, and reduce errors. The tradeoff: more sophisticated schemes need more complex receiver circuitry.

Digital Data → Analog Signal

Modulation Techniques (Keying)

When digital data must travel over a medium that only carries analog signals (e.g. optical fibre, radio, legacy telephone lines), the data is used to modulate one property of a carrier wave. The three fundamental techniques:

ASK — Amplitude Shift Keying
Binary values represented by different amplitudes of the carrier. Typically one amplitude is zero (presence/absence of carrier). Simple to implement but susceptible to noise. Up to 1200 bps on voice-grade lines. Used for digital data over optical fibre.
FSK — Frequency Shift Keying
Binary values represented by two different frequencies near the carrier. BFSK (Binary FSK) is the most common variant. Less susceptible to error than ASK. Up to 1200 bps on voice-grade lines; also used for high-frequency (3–30 MHz) radio. MFSK uses more than two frequencies for higher throughput.
PSK — Phase Shift Keying
Carrier phase is shifted to represent data. BPSK uses 2 phases (180° apart) for 2 binary values. QPSK uses 4 phases (90° apart), encoding 2 bits per symbol. Differential PSK encodes data as a phase change relative to the previous symbol rather than an absolute phase reference.

QAM — Quadrature Amplitude Modulation

A combination of ASK and PSK. Two copies of the carrier are used simultaneously, offset by 90°. Each copy is independently ASK-modulated, then the two are summed. This allows two independent bit streams to travel on the same carrier frequency, effectively doubling throughput. QAM is a logical extension of QPSK. Used in ADSL and many wireless standards.

Why PSK phases must be sufficiently separated: In BPSK the two phases are 180° apart. In QPSK, four phases are 90° apart. The larger the separation, the easier the receiver can distinguish them — but the fewer distinct levels you get for a given phase resolution. QAM overcomes this by adding amplitude variation on top of phase variation.
Analog Data → Digital Signal

Digitisation via Codec

Converting analog data (e.g. voice) to a digital bitstream is called digitisation. Done using a codec (coder-decoder). Two principal techniques:

Pulse Code Modulation (PCM)

Based on the Nyquist Sampling Theorem: if a signal is sampled at a rate greater than twice its highest frequency component, the samples contain all information needed to reconstruct the original signal exactly.

Voice is bandlimited to below 4000 Hz → minimum sampling rate = 8000 samples/second. Each sample is a continuous amplitude value (PAM — Pulse Amplitude Modulation sample). These samples are then quantised to discrete levels and assigned a binary code.

8-bit quantisation→256 discrete levels
8000 samples/s × 8 bits/sample→64 kbps for one voice channel
Quantisation error / noise: Mapping a continuous amplitude to a finite set of levels introduces approximation. The reconstructed signal is not identical to the original — the discrepancy is called quantisation noise. Finer quantisation (more bits per sample) reduces noise but increases data rate.

PCM pipeline: Continuous analog signal → PAM Sampler → Quantiser → Encoder → Digital bitstream. See block diagram at 25:51.

Delta Modulation

An alternative to PCM. The analog signal is approximated by a staircase function that steps up or down by a fixed amount at each sampling interval. The output is a single bit per interval: 1 if the staircase stepped up (analog signal exceeded the staircase), 0 if it stepped down. Two critical parameters are the step size and the sampling rate. Simpler than PCM but susceptible to slope overload (when the signal changes faster than the staircase can track). See waveform diagram at 26:36.

Analog Data → Analog Signal

Why Modulate Analog Data?

Raw analog signals (e.g. voice) occupy a low-frequency baseband range that suffers heavy attenuation on many media. Modulating onto a carrier shifts the signal into a frequency band the medium transmits faithfully.

Three variants:

AM — Amplitude Modulation FM — Frequency Modulation PM — Phase Modulation

AM and FM are the well-known radio broadcast standards. Voice over telephone lines is transmitted at its original spectrum (baseband) — no modulation needed when the channel already supports that frequency range. FDM (see below) relies on analog modulation to stack multiple voice channels onto a single wideband link.

Multiplexing & Demultiplexing

Core Concept

Multiplexing (muxing) combines multiple independent information streams for simultaneous transmission over a single shared link as one complex signal. Demultiplexing (demuxing) is the reverse — separating the combined signal back into its constituent streams at the receiver.

Types of Multiplexing

FDM — Frequency Division Multiplexing
Each sender is assigned a distinct sub-band (carrier frequency) within the total channel bandwidth. All sub-bands are transmitted simultaneously. Demultiplexer filters out each carrier and extracts the data. Classic example: AM/FM radio, cable TV, telephone trunk lines.
WDM — Wavelength Division Multiplexing
Conceptually identical to FDM but operates at the wavelength level over optical fibre. Multiple light beams at different wavelengths (colours) travel simultaneously through the same fibre. A prism (or diffraction grating) combines/separates them. WDM is essentially FDM for optical systems.
TDM — Time Division Multiplexing
The channel's time is divided into slots. Each sender is allocated repeating time slots in round-robin fashion. Two variants:

Synchronous TDM: Fixed slot assigned to each station regardless of whether it has data. Simple to decode but wastes bandwidth when a station is idle.

Statistical (Asynchronous) TDM: Slots are allocated dynamically only to stations that have data to send. More efficient channel utilisation but requires address/header info per slot so the demultiplexer knows which stream each slot belongs to.
CDM — Code Division Multiplexing
Does not divide by frequency or time. Each sender is assigned a unique binary code sequence called a chip sequence. Chip sequences are chosen to be orthogonal vectors — the dot product of any two different chip sequences is zero. Data from all senders is combined and transmitted simultaneously across the full bandwidth. The receiver, knowing a sender's chip sequence, can mathematically extract only that sender's data by computing the dot product with the combined signal.

The cell-phone variant is CDMA (Code Division Multiple Access). Also used in satellite communication.

Concept Summary

The Big Picture

Encoding maps data bits to signal elements to improve sync, reduce errors, and eliminate DC components. Complex encoding (Manchester, Differential Manchester) buys better synchronisation at the cost of higher modulation rate and more complex circuitry.

Modulation shifts a signal's frequency range by riding it on a carrier. Carrier frequency is chosen to match the channel's usable bandwidth. Digital data can be impressed onto analog carriers via ASK (amplitude), FSK (frequency), or PSK (phase). QAM combines amplitude and phase for higher throughput.

Digitisation (analog → digital) uses PCM (sample → quantise → encode) or Delta Modulation (staircase approximation). PCM at 8 bits / 8000 samples/s yields the standard 64 kbps voice channel. Quantisation always introduces noise — finer resolution costs more bits.

Multiplexing lets multiple data streams share one physical medium. FDM/WDM exploit the frequency/wavelength dimension; TDM exploits time; CDM exploits mathematical orthogonality. TDM and FDM are the most widely deployed.

Exam watchpoints: Know the four data/signal type combinations and which technique applies to each. Know that Manchester doubles the modulation rate. Know the Nyquist sampling rate formula (2 × fmax) and the PCM 64 kbps derivation. Know that WDM is FDM applied to optics. Know that CDM uses orthogonal chip sequences and dot products.
