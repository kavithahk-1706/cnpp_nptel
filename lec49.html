Lecture 49 medium priority

Data Link Layer — Ethernet

Continuation of the Ethernet deep-dive: frame structure, collision mechanics, addressing, and the full evolution from Standard → Bridged → Switched → Full-Duplex → Fast → Gigabit Ethernet.

Background

Ethernet Origins & Standards

Ethernet was developed by Bob Metcalfe and his team at Xerox PARC in the mid-1970s, with roots in the Aloha packet-radio network. It was standardised jointly by Xerox, DEC, and Intel in 1978.

Standard Speed MAC Policy Notes
IEEE 802.3 10 Mbps CSMA/CD Original "Standard Ethernet"
IEEE 802.3u 100 Mbps CSMA/CD Fast Ethernet; adds auto-negotiation
IEEE 802.3z 1,000 Mbps CSMA/CD Gigabit Ethernet; full-duplex in practice
IEEE 802.3ae 10 Gbps Full-duplex Ten-Gigabit; backbone use
Frame Structure

Ethernet Frame (IEEE 802.3)

The standard 802.3 frame has the following field layout. Note that some literature counts the Preamble as 8 bytes total; the standard separates it into 7-byte Preamble + 1-byte SFD.

Field Size Purpose
Preamble 7 bytes Informs the receiver a frame is arriving; enables clock synchronisation.
SFD (Start Frame Delimiter) 1 byte Signals that the Destination MAC field begins at the very next byte.
Destination MAC 6 bytes Identifies the receiving system.
Source MAC 6 bytes Identifies the sending system. Always a unicast address.
Type 2 bytes Identifies the higher-layer protocol encapsulated (e.g., IPv4, IPv6).
Data & Pad 46–1500 bytes Payload from the network layer. Padding added if data < 46 bytes to meet the 64-byte minimum frame requirement.
FCS (Frame Check Sequence) 4 bytes 32-bit CRC for detection of corrupted frames.
Header accounting: Excluding Preamble and SFD (8 bytes), the actual header is 14 bytes (Dest MAC + Src MAC + Type) at the front and 4 bytes (FCS) at the rear. The payload field therefore spans bytes 15 through (frame length − 4).

Why the 46-byte minimum payload?

This is a collision-detection constraint, not an arbitrary choice — see the Collision Detection section below for the full derivation.

Addressing

MAC Address & the NIC

Every network-enabled device has a Network Interface Card (NIC) that provides a unique 48-bit (6-byte) physical address burned into hardware. The NIC abstracts the physical medium — wired (coaxial, twisted-pair, fibre) or wireless (Wi-Fi, Bluetooth).

Finding your MAC: On Windows run ipconfig /all and look for "Physical Address". On Linux/macOS run ifconfig or ip link. The address appears as six hex pairs, e.g., 64:00:6A:1C:63:XX. (Demonstrated at 05:20 in the lecture video — worth watching for the live terminal output.)

At every hop, the MAC address must be re-resolved via ARP. The source and destination MAC fields in the frame change at each link; the IP addresses in the encapsulated datagram remain unchanged end-to-end.

Unicast, Multicast, and Broadcast

The least significant bit of the first byte of the destination address determines the address type.

Unicast
LSB of first byte = 0. Frame destined for exactly one station. Source MAC is always unicast.
Multicast
LSB of first byte = 1. Frame destined for a group of stations that have joined the multicast group.
Broadcast — a special case of multicast where all 48 bits are 1: FF:FF:FF:FF:FF:FF. Every station on the segment accepts and processes it.
Media Access Control

CSMA/CD — Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Detection

On shared-medium (half-duplex) Ethernet, two adaptors may simultaneously sense the channel as idle and begin transmitting, causing a collision. The adaptor detects this via an anomalous voltage difference on the wire.

Wireless note: Collision detection is not feasible in wireless media because a transmitting node cannot listen to the channel simultaneously. Wireless uses CSMA/CA (Collision Avoidance) instead — covered in a later lecture.

How does sender A know a collision occurred?

If A sends at time 0 and its frame reaches B at time T, then B's collision signal reaches A at time 2T. A must still be transmitting at 2T to hear the collision. IEEE 802.3 specifies the maximum value of 2T as 51.2 µs, corresponding to a maximum segment distance of 2,500 m at 10 Mbps.

At 10 Mbps it takes 0.1 µs to transmit 1 bit. In 51.2 µs, a total of 512 bits = 64 bytes can be transmitted. This is why the minimum Ethernet frame is 64 bytes.

Breakdown of the 64-byte minimum: 14 bytes header + 46 bytes data/pad + 4 bytes FCS = 64 bytes. If the payload is less than 46 bytes, padding is added.

Upon detecting a collision, the adaptor broadcasts a 48-bit jam signal to ensure all stations on the segment are aware of the collision before it fades.

Retransmission

Binary Exponential Backoff

After a collision, the sender does not retransmit immediately. It waits a random delay chosen from a progressively larger window to reduce the probability of repeated collisions.

Attempt (n) K drawn from Wait time
1st{0, 1}K × 51.2 µs
2nd{0, 1, 2, 3}K × 51.2 µs
nth{0 … 2ⁿ − 1}K × 51.2 µs
max K1023—

After approximately 16 failed attempts, the adaptor gives up and reports a transmit error to the host. A fixed (non-random) delay would cause senders to retransmit in lockstep, guaranteeing repeated collisions. A very small fixed window works for small networks but degrades severely under many nodes.

Receiver-side MAC algorithm

The receiver passively accepts all frames whose destination address matches one of four cases: addressed to its own unicast MAC, addressed to broadcast (FF:FF:FF:FF:FF:FF), addressed to a multicast group it belongs to, or all frames if the NIC is in promiscuous mode (used by packet sniffers/network analysers).

Limitations

Ethernet Issues (Traditional / Half-Duplex)

Low Peak Utilisation
Ethernet works best under light loads. Utilisation over ~30% is considered heavy because collisions waste bandwidth proportionally.
More Hosts → More Collisions
Each additional host increases the probability of simultaneous transmission, requiring more arbitration cycles.
Longer Cables → Worse Recovery
Larger 2T means the sender must hold the channel longer during collision detection, wasting more bandwidth per event.
Practical Limits
Specification allows up to 1,024 hosts; real deployments cap at ~200 for acceptable performance. RTT is typically 5–10 µs on short segments.

Transport-layer flow control (TCP window management) partially mitigates collision pressure by reducing the number of back-to-back packets. Ethernet is nonetheless considered inexpensive, fast, and easy to administer relative to alternatives.

Evolution

How Ethernet Evolved

Bridged Ethernet

A bridge is a Layer 2 device placed between two LAN segments. It creates separate collision domains on each side — collisions in segment A cannot propagate to segment B. This effectively doubles usable bandwidth and is the foundational idea behind the modern switch.

Switched Ethernet (Multiport Bridge)

A switch is simply a bridge with many ports — each port is its own collision domain. A 12-port switch creates 12 isolated collision domains. Switches can be arranged hierarchically (access switches → distribution switch → router) to scale large campuses. The switch learns MAC addresses dynamically and forwards unicast frames only to the correct port.

Full-Duplex Ethernet

In full-duplex mode, each station is connected to the switch via two separate wires: one dedicated transmit line and one dedicated receive line. Because transmit and receive never share a medium, collisions are physically impossible.

Key consequence: Full-duplex Ethernet requires no CSMA/CD. The MAC layer is dramatically simplified. A 10 Mbps link effectively becomes 20 Mbps aggregate (10 Mbps each direction simultaneously). To support flow and error control in this new model, a MAC Control sublayer is inserted between the LLC sublayer and the MAC sublayer.

Fast Ethernet (802.3u) — 100 Mbps

Designed to compete with FDDI and Fibre Channel. Fast Ethernet is backward-compatible with Standard Ethernet: same 48-bit addressing, same frame format, same min/max frame length. The only physical-layer change is the encoding scheme (4B5B). Many NICs support 10/100 auto-sensing.

The key new feature is auto-negotiation, which lets two connected devices automatically agree on the best common mode and data rate. Its three objectives are: allow incompatible-speed devices to interoperate, allow a device to advertise multiple capabilities, and allow a station to query the switch/hub capability.

Gigabit Ethernet (802.3z) — 1,000 Mbps

Compatible with lower Ethernet speeds. Uses standard framing and CSMA/CD algorithm (though in practice always full-duplex). Maximum cable length is determined by signal attenuation, not collision timing. Primarily deployed for backbone and inter-router connectivity. Distance is severely limited compared to lower-speed variants.

Ten-Gigabit Ethernet (802.3ae) — 10 Gbps is the current generation, used exclusively for backbone links. Higher speeds carry increased cost and management complexity; most end-user applications cannot saturate even 100 Mbps, making high-speed uplinks a backbone concern rather than an access concern.

Concept Summary

Key Takeaways

64-byte minimum frame
Derived from the 2T propagation constraint at 10 Mbps over 2,500 m. 14 bytes header + 46 bytes data/pad + 4 bytes FCS = 64 bytes. Padding fills the gap when payload < 46 bytes.
Jam signal
After detecting a collision via voltage anomaly, the sender emits a 48-bit jam signal to ensure every node on the segment is aware before it stops transmitting.
Exponential backoff
Randomised, growing wait window prevents retransmission lockstep. Maximum K = 1023 slots; give up after ~16 attempts.
MAC address types
Determined by LSB of first byte of destination: 0 = unicast, 1 = multicast. All-ones (FF:FF:FF:FF:FF:FF) = broadcast, a special multicast case.
Full-duplex eliminates CSMA/CD
Separate TX/RX wires per station mean no shared medium and no collisions. MAC Control sublayer added for flow and error control.
Auto-negotiation (Fast Ethernet)
Allows devices of different speeds/modes to advertise capabilities and agree on the best common operating mode automatically.
